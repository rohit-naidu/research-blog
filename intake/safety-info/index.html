Safety review

I. Endocrine safety: MTC and MEN 2

The need for the question

Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) carry a black-box warning regarding the risk of thyroid C-cell tumors. While this risk was primarily observed in rodent models, personal or family histories of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2) are absolute contraindications based on FDA product labeling.

Practical steps based on answer

  • If yes: GLP-1 therapy must be avoided entirely.
  • Clinician action: Transition the patient to non-incretin-based therapies, such as phentermine-topiramate for “Hungry Brain” or naltrexone-bupropion for “Emotional Eating” phenotypes.

II. Pancreatobiliary protection: Pancreatitis and gallbladder disease

The need for the question

Clinical trials and post-marketing surveillance indicate a low but significant risk (<1%) of acute pancreatitis and gallbladder-related events. Rapid weight loss (>2 lbs/week) and reduced dietary fat can increase bile lithogenicity, leading to gallstones and cholecystitis.

Practical steps based on answer

  • If history of pancreatitis: Therapy represents a major relative contraindication. Order baseline serum lipase before initiation and educate the patient on red-flag abdominal symptoms.
  • If gallbladder attacks/stones: Utilize a CCK-HIDA scan to determine the gallbladder ejection fraction (EF).
  • Clinician action: If EF is <35% (biliary dyskinesia), avoid rapid weight-loss profiles and mandate a low-fat diet to prevent acute cholecystitis.

III. Motility and gastric emptying: Gastroparesis and obstruction

The need for the question

The primary therapeutic mechanism of GLP-1 RAs is the slowing of gastric emptying. In patients with pre-existing gastroparesis or a history of bowel obstruction, GLP-1 exposure can exacerbate subclinical delays into pathological stomach paralysis.

Practical steps based on answer

  • If yes: In patients with prior motility complaints, perform a solid-phase gastric emptying study using radioactive tracer scintigraphy.
  • Clinician action: If retained gastric content is >10% at 4 hours, avoid GLP-1 therapy. For milder cases, utilize a microdosing protocol (starting at 10–25% of standard dose) to allow the gut to build tolerance gradually.

IV. Genetic proxies for emetic sensitivity: Repeated vomiting

The need for the question

A 2026 landmark study in Nature (Su et al.) confirmed that variations in GLP1R (rs10305420) and GIPR (rs1800437) are associated with nausea and vomiting on incretin therapy. A history of severe, multi-day vomiting (for example in response to anesthesia) can serve as a clinical proxy for higher emetic sensitivity.

Practical steps based on answer

  • If yes: The patient may be at substantially higher risk of severe, persistent vomiting on dual agonists such as tirzepatide. Genetic effects are modest; clinical context and dose still matter most.
  • Clinician action: Consider a symptom-guided microdosing protocol. Titrate at extended 6–8 week intervals and use a hold-and-reduce approach: if vomiting occurs, return to the previous tolerated dose for 8 weeks.

V. Renal and volume maintenance: Dehydration and kidney problems

The need for the question

GLP-1 RAs are primarily excreted via the kidneys. Severe gastrointestinal side effects (vomiting/diarrhea) can lead to acute volume depletion, which has been linked to acute kidney injury (AKI) in vulnerable populations.

Practical steps based on answer

  • If yes: Establish baseline creatinine and eGFR levels.
  • Clinician action: Mandate aggressive electrolyte and fluid loading (2–2.5 L/day). Avoid dose escalation if the patient cannot maintain hydration.

VI. Life stage and special comorbidities: Pregnancy and eye disease

The need for the question

  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: GLP-1s are not recommended due to lack of safety data and established animal model risks. FDA labeling requires cessation at least two months prior to conception.
  • Diabetes eye disease: Rapid improvements in HbA1c can sometimes lead to a transient worsening of diabetic retinopathy.

Practical steps based on answer

  • If planning pregnancy: Advise the patient to defer therapy or establish a clear exit ramp four months before the attempt.
  • If eye disease present: Ensure a baseline fundoscopic exam and utilize a more gradual titration to avoid abrupt glycemic shifts.
